Bright Bots Factory

Cyberpunk - Puzzle solving - Story

About the Game

As part of the Progressive Gamejam, put on by the Sacramento SquareOne club, Bright Bots is a puzzle solving story game assembled by our indie group, Pretty Neat Games. Set in the cyberpunk setting, using the Unity Engine, you're an unpaid computer hardware intern working for the up and coming Bright Bots Robots factory as their lead designer.

You are underappreciated by your boss and your work is scrutanized constantly by the media, but your work can change the world..for better or worse. Players build and design robot CPU's using a variety of shape and color piece to accomidate for a particular task OR intentionally misdesign robots to cause havoic & prevent yourself from being replaced by bots themselves.

Game Objectives

Core Mechanics

Each level has five rounds in where you are given a bot, an assembly line of pieces, instructions for what to build, and a time limit for each round. On the assembly line you are given pieces needed to assemble together a robot's cpu. By mouse clicking a piece, you can pick it up and place it on the octagon to earn points towards a specific color requirement. Once all requirements are met, you can Submit the bot, stating its ready for the market.

The primary pieces are broken up into three categories of colors:

Red is for Strength, a major stat in robots requiring alot of physical strength or high cpu usage. Such bots might include Construction Bots or Data Mining Bots.

Yellow is for Personality, a core feature of bots that need to be user friendly or be able to adapt to human behavior. Such bots might include Nanny Bots or Taxi Bots.

Blue is for Intellegence, programming the learning capacity of bots to preform advanced calculations or being able to relay orders to other bots. Such bots might include Engineer Bots or Manager Bots.

Each piece is randomly given a shape when placed on the assembly line and play a core part to assembling the bot's cpu.

Pieces are placed inside the bot's head with lines outlining where pieces can be placed. If a newly placed piece would collide with an already existing piece, it is placed back on the conveyor.


Losing isn't always a bad thing

Though sometimes not ideal, you can always Submit a bot before it meets all the requirements. What do you got to lose? You don't get paid anyways!

By doing this, you can conserve pieces needed for more advanced pieces on later rounds at the cost of opening yourself up to Scrutany by the Media and your Boss.

Throughout the story mode of the game you are prompted with offers by your boss and other competing companies to Sabotage your bots for a benifit like extra pieces or Super-charged pieces .

By designing Ideal Bots however, you also gain access to a level of protection against Scrutany from the Media, so they will be more forgiving of your mistakes.

In addition, there are times in the story that assembled bots will replace your co-workers and potentially you if you craft too many well designed bots, so you are encouraged to dabbled between producing Ideal Bots and Sabotaging them.


Dev's Corner

Initial Mockup

When we started all we had was an idea and a photoshoped mockup of what the basic shapes would look like.

In our first iteration of the game, our pieces didn't have texture or much in the way of art. At the SquareOne meetups there seemed to be an abundance of programmers but very very first artists. It wouldn't be until a couple months into development would we be able to pick up our first career artist.


Our first level

When we were able to assemble the conveyor we got to developing our very first game level. It was very basic and could easily bugged out by clicking off the edges of the Octagon, but damn it, it was something. It was at this time that our Lead Story Designer was able to craft nicely made pieces with texture. This gave us great hope for what this game could be!

With the new pieces in game, this project started to look like an actual game.


The Name of the Game

Originally we went with the name that its creator chose, PuzzleBot embedding the core features of the game in the name to immediately give onlookers a complete idea of what it would be about. Solving puzzles to create bots!

Over time, we begain to pitch other names, seeing as Puzzle Bot was mostly copyrighted. One of them being Assembly Tech. It never stook, but it was a major part of development establishing the name.

Finally, as we got the main game mode down and started assembling our menus, we came up with the name that would both stand for the title of the game but also the name of the company you would work for:

Bright Bots Factory


Inexperience doesn't mean Undetermined

For many of us, this was our first actual game project, using a real game engine and having team meetings to ship this game. Because of this, the system design of the game wasn't very fleshed out initially and rehawls were needed. Despite this, every advancement was exhilarating and we would travel to play test and present the game to local indie shows.

Without a doubt there were dozens of bugs through development, not to mention some unbeatable levels or broken UI, but that didn't seem to phase our developers.
